Understanding the French school system

Learn how the French public education system is organized and how it may differ from what you’re used to.

Expand the sections below for more details:


  • The French school system stands out for its structure, formality, and national consistency. Here are a few key features that set it apart


    • School starts earlier: French children start school at age 3 (école maternelle).

    • Long school days: Days run from 8:30am to 4:30pm, with no school on Wednesdays.

    • Lunch breaks: Usually last 2 hours and are designed to give children time for a proper, sit-down meal and time to play afterwards.

    • Frequent breaks: There are two-week holidays every 6 - 7 weeks.

    • Formal style: Teaching tends to be more academic and less focused on play, even in early years.

    • National curriculum: All public schools follow the same national program.


  • The French school year is divided into several holiday periods:


    • Autumn Break: A two-week holiday at the end of October.

    • Christmas Break: A two-week holiday around Christmas and New Year.

    • Winter Break: A two-week holiday in February.

    • Spring Break: A two-week holiday in April.

    • Summer Break: A long holiday starting in early July and lasting until the beginning of September.

    There are also several public holidays where students don’t attend school, like Easter, May 1st, All Saints' Day, etc.


    France is divided in 3 zones for childrens’ holidays and each one has its own break time.



  • In the French elementary school system, students' progress is regularly evaluated through various methods:


    • Continuous Assessment: Teachers observe and assess students’ overall development, including social skills, effort, and behavior. The focus is on the child’s individual growth rather than on specific grades.

    • Oral Feedback: Instead of formal grades, teachers provide verbal feedback to students, focusing on strengths and areas for improvement. Feedback is typically given in class and occasionally shared with parents.

    • Reports: Teachers issue progress reports at the end of each term, giving feedback on the child’s strengths, areas for improvement, and overall progress.

    • Parent-Teacher Meetings: At the Robert Schuman there is typically one meeting at the beginning of the school year.

    • End-of-Year Evaluation: At the end of the school year, teachers assess whether the child is ready to move to the next grade level. Special attention is given to students who might need additional support.

    • During the school year, other meetings can be requested by parents or teachers.

  • Parental involvement in French public schools tends to be more limited compared to some other countries:


    • Basic Communication: Families are expected to stay informed via school newsletters, notes in the child’s agenda, and occasional messages from teachers.

    • Parent Associations: Voluntary participation in associations allows families to be represented in the school council and engage.

    • Helping at Events: Parents may be invited to assist with school trips, swimming lessons, or special events. Participation is appreciated but not required.

    • Homework Supervision: While the school doesn’t place a heavy academic burden on families, regular homework supervision at home is expected.


    • School Changes: If a family moves or requests a transfer, the child can change schools, usually without academic testing, though administrative approval may be needed.

    • National Evaluations: Some national assessments take place in CP (1st grade) and CE1 (2nd grade) to help teachers identify students’ needs, but these are not pass/fail tests and don’t affect school placement.

    • Private or Specialized Schools: If transferring to a private or specialized school, individual schools may have their own criteria or informal evaluations.

    • Transition to the Collège International de l'Esplanade (middle school). École Élémentaire Internationale Robert Schuman is a feeder school for the Collège International de l’Esplanade. However, students still need to apply and pass entrance exams in the target language.


    • Teacher Communication: Parents are encouraged to speak with the teacher to understand the child's challenges and work together on solutions.

    • Personalized Support: Teachers can put in place support systems such as individualized help during or outside school hours to assist the child.

    • School Support Teams: If difficulties persist, the school may involve a support team (specialized teachers and psychologists) to assess and provide targeted help.

    • Repeating a Grade: In some cases, repeating a school year may be proposed, but this is discussed carefully with the family, isn’t automatic, and highly unlikely.

Language classes, support sessions for children,
parent associations, and more

École Élémentaire Internationale Robert Schuman in Strasbourg offers support for students and families who do not speak French fluently.


Dedicated language programs and international sections help ease the transition into the French school system, while various parent associations can assist with communication and school-related matters.

More detailed information below:


  • Yes, the École Élémentaire Internationale Robert Schuman in Strasbourg provides dedicated support for students who are not fluent in French.


    • The school hosts a specialized class known as Unité Pédagogique pour Élèves Allophones Arrivants, designed to assist non-French-speaking students. This program offers intensive French language instruction.

    • International sections (German, British, Spanish, and Italian) aim to facilitate the integration of foreign students into the French educational system and support their potential reintegration into their home country's education system.

    • If difficulties persist, the school may call on the Réseau d'Aides Spécialisées aux Élèves en Difficulté - a team of specialized teachers and school psychologists who provide targeted support for students in need.


    • The school offers Activités pédagogiques complémentaires - short support sessions in small groups held outside of regular class hours. These sessions focus on helping students with specific difficulties, such as reading, writing, or math.

    • For students with more significant needs, the school may create a Programme personnalisé pour la Réussite Scolaire. This plan is developed in collaboration with the teaching staff and parents to set appropriate learning goals and tailored strategies.

    • International sections (British, German, Spanish, and Italian) - in these sections teachers often have some knowledge of the section's language.

    • Parent Associations: The school has an active parent association, FCPE, which includes members from diverse linguistic backgrounds. This association can assist in bridging communication gaps between non-French-speaking parents and the school staff.
      Other associations are: Apelevis and Liste Indépendante.

    • Direct Communication: For specific concerns or to discuss your child's progress, you can directly contact the school administration: ce.0672269C@ac-strasbourg.fr

Meal options, dietary needs, commuting to school, and more

Meals and transportation at École Élémentaire Internationale Robert Schuman are managed in coordination with the City of Strasbourg.

Lunchtime is considered extracurricular and families must register and pay for the service. While there is no school bus, the school is easily accessible by public transport and bike.

More details below:

QUICK LINKS
School canteen menus School canteen registration

  • In French public schools, lunchtime is considered extracurricular because it happens outside official class hours and is managed by the local municipality, not the school.

    It's optional, supervised by non-teaching staff, and families must register and pay for the service.


  • The canteen is managed by the City of Strasbourg and offers four meal options:


    • Standard meal
    • Standard meal without pork
    • Halal meal
    • Vegetarian meal

    Menus are planned in advance and can be consulted on the official Strasbourg city website.


    • École Élémentaire Internationale Robert Schuman in Strasbourg does not allow students to bring their own lunch from home.

    • If your child has specific dietary needs or preferences, it would be best to contact the school directly to discuss possible accommodations.


  • The school does not provide a dedicated school bus service. Parents are responsible for arranging their children's transportation to and from school.


    However, the school is well-connected to public transportation, making commuting convenient for families, as is the case in many other French cities.
    Commuting by bike is very popular in Strasbourg and there are many bike racks in front of the school.


    • Drop-off and pick-up take place at the school entrances. Parents should check with the teacher to confirm which gate applies to their child.

    • There is a small public parking area right in front of the school, but spaces are limited.


  • For children with specific allergies or medical dietary needs, parents must establish a Projet d’Accueil Individualisé (PAI) with the City of Strasbourg. This plan outlines the necessary accommodations for the child's health requirements.


    • In some cases, the PAI may allow families to provide a complete packed meal for their child.

    • To arrange a PAI, contact the school's extracurricular services at: periscolaireeteducatif-robertschuman@strasbourg.eu

    For more information on school health services, visit strasbourg.eu/sante-ecole.


  • If someone other than a parent or legal guardian will pick up your child, you must provide written authorization. This allows the school to ensure safety.


    Please inform your child's teacher or the school administration in advance, supplying the full name and relationship of the authorized person.

School events and activities, peer issues, fostering friendships, and more

Children from all backgrounds are welcomed into an inclusive and supportive school environment. Integration is encouraged through extracurricular activities and collaboration between teachers, families, and the school health team.

If your child has trouble adjusting or making friends, dedicated support is available to help them feel at ease.

More information below:


  • Fostering friendships is integral to your child's integration and well-being. Here are some ways you can support this process:


    • Encourage Participation in Extracurricular Activities: Engaging in after-school clubs or sports can provide opportunities for your child to interact with peers in a relaxed setting.

    • Promote Social Interaction: Arrange playdates or group activities outside of school to help your child build relationships in different environments.

    • Communicate with Teachers: Maintain open communication with your child's teachers to stay informed about their social interactions and to address any concerns promptly.

    • Be Supportive and Patient: Adjusting to a new social environment can take time. Offer encouragement and be patient as your child navigates new friendships.

  • If your child is experiencing difficulties with classmates, consider the following steps:


    • Initiate Communication: : Reach out to your child's teacher to discuss the situation. Teachers can provide insights into your child's interactions and may suggest strategies to improve social integration.

    • School Health Team: The school collaborates with health professionals who support the educational staff in addressing students' health-related needs, including psychological well-being.

    • Promote Social Activities: : Encourage your child to participate in extracurricular activities offered by the school. These activities provide opportunities for students to interact in diverse settings, which can help in building friendships and improving social skills.

  • The school encourages parental involvement and offers various avenues for parents to engage with the school community:


    • Parent-Teacher Meetings at the beginning of the school year provide an opportunity to meet teachers and connect with other parents.

    • School Events and Activities: The school organizes events such as cultural days, workshops, etc., where parents can participate and interact with other families.

    • FCPE: The local branch of the national parent association is active in supporting parents and students at the school. They facilitate communication between parents and the school administration, advocate for students' rights, and organize events, etc.
      To get in touch with FCPE members, please write to

Dealing with chronic conditions and allergies, PAI, emergency situations

The school prioritizes your child’s health and safety throughout the day. Staff are trained to respond quickly to illness or injury, whether minor or serious. Families are encouraged to share any medical conditions in advance so appropriate care plans can be arranged.

See below for more information on procedures and emergency contacts:

Email to arrange PAI → School health services →

  • If your child becomes ill or sustains an injury during school hours, the staff will take immediate and appropriate action.


    • Minor Illnesses or Injuries: For minor issues, such as a mild headache or small scrape, the school staff will provide basic first aid and monitor your child's condition.

    • Serious Illnesses or Injuries: In cases of more serious health concerns, the school will promptly contact you using the emergency contact information you have provided. If necessary, they will also contact emergency medical services to ensure your child receives appropriate care.

    It's important to keep the school informed of any medical conditions your child may have and to ensure that the school has up-to-date emergency contact information.


    • If your child has medical needs such as chronic conditions, allergies, or requires medication at school, you’ll need to set up a Projet d’Accueil Individualisé (PAI).

    • To start, contact the school and provide medical documentation from your doctor. The PAI is created together with the family, school staff, and the school health team, and it details how your child will be supported - this may include medication, dietary restrictions, or activity adjustments.

From current parents to new parents: why we created this page

Navigating the school system as a new parent can be challenging, especially when clear and accessible information is lacking.

The school’s website may not always provide key details, regular updates, or clear procedures, making it difficult for parents to find contact information or get answers.

Busy parents often resort to multiple emails, phone calls, or in-person visits, adding stress for both families and school staff.

In addition, the French public school system doesn’t allow visits or share detailed information before admission, making the transition even harder.

This webpage was created to bridge these gaps, offering a centralized, user-friendly resource to help new parents feel informed, supported, and connected.
